How does weather explain New York shooting rates?

For Data Incubator Project, I apply Bayesian Multilevel to model the rates of shooting in the City of Greater New York. While the second level of the model pools information across space (based on census data, the social and economic background), the first level simulate the trend and variability in response to the daily weather. This arrangement allows us to explore the variability of response with regard to the different social/economic background. If time permits, at the later stages of this investigation, I incorporate the effect of air quality (using AQI) to inform the rates of gun violence.

Data: For this analysis, I use multiple sources of information. This includes NYPD shooting rate dataset (2006-2018), NCDC daily weather data, NYC census data (American Community Survey - ACS DP03 and DP05 tables). Later, I will include the AQI (Air Quality Index) to study the relevancy of air pollution to the occurrence of shooting. The time-span of observation considered in this analysis is 2006-2018.

The Bayesian Hierarchical Model: Zero-Inflated Poisson Regression Method

The Socioeconomic status in five boroughs of the greater New York City diversifies, but the precincts inside each borough might be similar. Therefore, rather than aggregating data over each borough, the model benefits from the social/economic background to pool the shooting rates over precincts and capture their variability response to different predictors, including time and weather. In this analysis, we look at the 144 months of data for 76 precincts across New York City boroughs. The model provides a mixtures of a Poisson and Bernoulli probability mass function to allow more flexibility in simulating the probability of zero outcomes.

The model implments on JAGS to apply Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC):

  • First level infroms the shooting rates with time+weather varaibles
  • Second level applies social/economic background including Poverty, Employment and Income.
  • The last level infroms priors in the Bayesian model
# This model runs on JAGS

model <- function() {
  
  for (m in 1:n_PRECINCT) #loop through all Police Precincts
  {
    for(i in 1:ntimes){ #loop through all data points
      y[i,m] ~ dpois(mu[i,m])
      mu[i,m] <- lambda[i,m]*z[i,m] + 0.0001 ## hack required for Rjags -- otherwise 'incompatible'-error
      z[i,m] ~ dbern(psi[m])
      log(lambda[i,m]) <- alpha[m] + beta_time[m]*time[i] + beta_prcp[m]*PRCP[i] + beta_tmax[m]*TMAX[i]
      
      # alpha is overall intercept and betas are liner effect
    }
  }
  
  # priors:
  
  for (m in 1:n_PRECINCT)
  {
    
    alpha[m] ~ dnorm(a_alpha[m]+b_alpha[m]*Poverty[m]+c_alpha[m]*Unemployment[m]+d_alpha[m]*IncomePerCap[m]+e_alpha[m]*Citizen[m],0.01)
    
    a_alpha[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    b_alpha[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    c_alpha[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    d_alpha[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    e_alpha[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    
    beta_time[m] ~ dnorm(a_beta1[m]+b_beta1[m]*Poverty[m]+c_beta1[m]*Unemployment[m]+d_beta1[m]*IncomePerCap[m]+e_beta1[m]*Citizen[m],0.01)
    beta_prcp[m] ~ dnorm(a_beta2[m]+b_beta2[m]*Poverty[m]+c_beta2[m]*Unemployment[m]+d_beta2[m]*IncomePerCap[m]+e_beta2[m]*Citizen[m],0.01)
    beta_tmax[m] ~ dnorm(a_beta3[m]+b_beta3[m]*Poverty[m]+c_beta3[m]*Unemployment[m]+d_beta3[m]*IncomePerCap[m]+e_beta3[m]*Citizen[m],0.01)
    
    a_beta1[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    b_beta1[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    c_beta1[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    d_beta1[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    e_beta1[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    
    a_beta2[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    b_beta2[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    c_beta2[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    d_beta2[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    e_beta2[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    
    a_beta3[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    b_beta3[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    c_beta3[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    d_beta3[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    e_beta3[m] ~ dnorm(0, 0.01)
    
    psi[m] ~ dunif(0, 1) # proportion of non-zeros
    
  }
}
